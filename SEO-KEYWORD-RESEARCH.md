# Keyword research — what teachers actually type

Pulled directly from Google's autocomplete API (real query data, not guesses) plus a competitive audit of GPTZero, Draftback, Process Feedback, and PlagiarismCheck.org. Goal: find the exact phrases teachers search, and map them to Inspect / StyleMatch / Verify / Citations.

## 1. The dominant, hard-to-win cluster: "AI detector"

Autocomplete for **"ai detector for teachers"**: free, grammarly, reddit, app, online, tagalog, "ai detection for teachers."
Autocomplete for **"chatgpt detector"**: free, grammarly, turnitin, reddit, remover, scribbr, image, pdf, zero.

This term is owned by GPTZero (17M users, 1M educators, massive PR/press wall) and Turnitin (built into most LMSs already). Ranking #1 here isn't realistic for a new site. Competing head-on on "AI detector" would also cut against your own positioning — you've deliberately never called PaperTrail an AI detector.

**The wedge:** teachers are searching *because they don't trust these tools.*

Autocomplete for **"ai detector false positive"**: false positive rate, reddit, "ai detection false positive neurodivergent," "turnitin ai detection false positive," "copyleaks ai detector false positive." That neurodivergent/ESL-bias angle showed up independently in the GPTZero FAQ too (they specifically claim to de-bias for ESL writers) — it's a known pain point, not a fringe search.

This is a real, searchable, low-competition angle: content that honestly explains *why* probability-based AI detectors produce false positives, and positions "look at the process, not a score" as the alternative — which is your actual product, not a repositioning.

## 2. Direct competitive territory: Google Docs revision history

Autocomplete for **"google docs revision history"**: extension, mobile, not showing, playback, video, **ai**, app, "version history."

"google docs revision history ai" appearing in autocomplete on its own is a strong, direct signal — people are already typing exactly what Inspect does.

Your direct competitors here are Draftback (500K users, $ subscription) and Process Feedback (free, and its meta tags explicitly target `"draftback alternative"` and `"revision history extension"` — they're actively fighting for this exact query). Inspect is also free, which is your strongest angle against Draftback specifically.

Autocomplete for **"draftback alternative"** returns the term itself plus "draftback free alternative" and "draftback extension alternative" — low volume, but high intent and low competition. Worth owning outright since almost nobody targets it besides Process Feedback.

## 3. "Plagiarism checker" — conflated with AI detection in teachers' heads

Autocomplete for **"plagiarism checker for teachers"**: free, best, **ai plagiarism checker for teachers**, "free plagiarism checker for teachers and students," turnitin, "for professors," "teachers use."

Teachers don't cleanly separate "plagiarism" (copied text) from "AI-written" (original but not theirs) in how they search — "ai plagiarism checker" is a real, common query even though it's a bit of a contradiction in terms. This is exactly the confusion your no-verdict, no-plagiarism-detector positioning is built to clarify — which makes it good FAQ material rather than something to avoid.

## 4. Authorship / style comparison — StyleMatch's lane

No autocomplete volume on exact phrases (long-tail, low search count), but real competitors exist targeting this conceptually: PlagiarismCheck.org's "Fingerprint" tool (8-component stylometric comparison — nearly identical concept to StyleMatch's 8 metrics) and a smaller player called "Authorship Authentication." Terms worth having on-page even without huge search volume, since they're what a teacher would type after already deciding they want this *type* of tool: "authorship verification," "writing style comparison," "does this sound like my student's writing."

## 5. Top-of-funnel guide content (this is where the real traffic is)

Autocomplete for **"how to tell if a student used ai"**: "...to write an essay," "...to write a paper," "...on a paper," "...reddit," plus "how to check/determine/find out if a student used ai." This cluster has by far the most query variations and the most existing competing content (I found 7+ "Teacher's Guide" articles from GPTZero, Proofademic, ThesifY, etc. all targeting it) — meaning there's proven demand, but also proven competition.

This is a content-marketing play, not a product-page play: a genuinely useful, non-salesy guide page ("How to tell if a student used AI — a teacher's guide") that answers the question honestly, then naturally introduces Inspect/StyleMatch/Verify as *how* you'd gather that evidence rather than guess. GPTZero's `/educators` page is the model to beat — but it's sales-forward where yours could be more credible by being genuinely more balanced.
